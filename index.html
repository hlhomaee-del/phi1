Concept 1 — What Is HIPAA?

HIPAA = The law that protects patient health information

  • Enacted in 1996 — Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act
  • Sets national standards for protecting patient privacy, security, and confidentiality
  • Applies to covered entities: healthcare providers, health plans, and healthcare clearinghouses
  • Also applies to business associates — companies that handle PHI on behalf of covered entities
  • Failure to comply = civil AND criminal penalties

Concept 2 — What Is PHI?

PHI = Protected Health Information

PHI is any individually identifiable health information held or transmitted by a covered entity or business associate. It includes information that:

  • Relates to a person's past, present, or future physical or mental health condition
  • Relates to the provision of healthcare to that individual
  • Relates to past, present, or future payment for healthcare
  • AND can be used to identify the individual

Key point: Health information WITHOUT identifiers is NOT PHI (e.g., anonymous data in a research study).

Concept 3 — Confidentiality

Confidentiality = Keeping patient information private

  • Only those with a need to know (treatment, payment, operations) may access PHI
  • Information must not be shared with unauthorized individuals — including family, friends, coworkers without need
  • Staff must use the minimum necessary amount of information for the task at hand
  • Confidentiality applies to verbal, written, and electronic forms of PHI
  • A conversation about a patient in a public hallway = confidentiality violation

Concept 4 — Who Needs to Know HIPAA?

Everyone who touches patient information must understand HIPAA

  • Front desk staff — collect, verify, and handle patient intake information
  • Billing specialists — process claims using diagnoses, insurance info, and identifiers
  • Coding specialists — access medical records and documentation to assign codes
  • Records staff — manage, store, and release health records
  • IT staff — maintain systems that store and transmit electronic PHI

The CBCS exam tests that you understand HIPAA applies to your role as a billing/coding specialist — not just to clinicians.

Concept 5 — TPO: The Three Permitted Uses

HIPAA allows PHI use for Treatment, Payment & Healthcare Operations

  • Treatment — sharing PHI with other providers involved in care (e.g., referral to specialist)
  • Payment — using PHI to submit insurance claims, verify eligibility, obtain payment
  • Healthcare Operations — quality assurance, staff training, compliance audits, administrative functions

Key rule: PHI used for TPO purposes generally does NOT require a separate patient authorization. For other purposes — marketing, research, etc. — specific authorization IS needed.

Concept 6 — Two Rules, One Goal

HIPAA has two main rules that billing & coding staff must understand

Think of them this way:

  • Privacy Rule = Governs use and disclosure of all forms of PHI (paper, verbal, electronic)
  • Security Rule = Protects electronic PHI (ePHI) specifically through safeguards

The Privacy Rule is the broader umbrella. The Security Rule is a more specific subset that focuses on technology and electronic systems.

Concept 7 — The Three Security Rule Safeguard Categories

Administrative · Physical · Technical

The HIPAA Security Rule requires covered entities to implement three types of safeguards to protect ePHI:

  • Administrative Safeguards — Policies, procedures, training, risk assessments, workforce management
  • Physical Safeguards — Locks, facility access controls, workstation security, device and media controls
  • Technical Safeguards — Passwords, encryption, audit logs, automatic logoff, access controls in software

Memory trick: Admin = People & Paper rules · Physical = Buildings & Devices · Technical = Software & Systems

Concept 8 — The Compliance Framework

Four practical layers of HIPAA compliance

HIPAA compliance is not just one rule — it works in layers. Tap each layer to expand it.

🔵 Layer 1: Protect PHI & Maintain Confidentiality
The foundation: patient health information is private. Only those with a legitimate need may access it. Minimum necessary applies. Unauthorized access or disclosure = violation.
🟢 Layer 2: Follow Rules for Using & Disclosing PHI
The Privacy Rule governs when PHI can be used or disclosed. TPO uses are permitted. Other uses generally require patient authorization. Release must be to authorized individuals only.
🟣 Layer 3: Protect ePHI Through Technical & Physical Safeguards
The Security Rule requires administrative, physical, and technical safeguards for electronic PHI. Risk analysis must be performed. Vulnerabilities must be identified and addressed.
🟠 Layer 4: Support Compliance Through Training, Auditing & Monitoring
Ongoing compliance requires workforce training, routine monitoring of access logs, regular audits, and enforcement of sanction policies when violations occur. Compliance is proactive, not just reactive.

Concept 9 — What Is Release of Information?

Release of Information = sharing PHI with parties outside the organization

  • Disclosure = releasing PHI to someone outside the covered entity (external release)
  • Before releasing PHI, the office must verify: who is requesting, why, and whether required documentation is complete
  • Even family members are NOT automatically entitled to a patient's PHI
  • Releasing PHI to an unauthorized party = HIPAA violation, regardless of intent
  • The office should follow both HIPAA requirements AND facility policy

Concept 10 — What Documentation Is Required?

Before releasing PHI, verify that required documentation is complete and valid

  • Authorization form — must be signed by the patient or their legal representative
  • What a valid authorization must include:
    • Patient's name and identifying information
    • Description of the information to be released (specific, not vague)
    • Name of the person or organization authorized to receive it
    • Purpose of the disclosure
    • Expiration date or event
    • Patient's signature and date
    • Statement about right to revoke
  • An incomplete form = do not release until corrected

Concept 11 — Authorized vs Unauthorized Release

Not everyone who asks has the right to receive PHI

  • Authorized release — valid documentation is complete, recipient is identified, purpose is legitimate, patient has consented or law permits
  • Unauthorized release — no signed authorization, recipient is not verified, information exceeds what was permitted, or facility policy was bypassed
  • Even well-intentioned releases without proper documentation = violations
  • Verify identity AND authority before releasing — especially by phone, fax, or email
